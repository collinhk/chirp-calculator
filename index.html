About Dolbear's Law

In 1897, Amos Emerson Dolbear — a physics professor at Tufts University and amateur naturalist — published a short paper in The American Naturalist titled "The Cricket as a Thermometer." Dolbear had noticed something that many people had vaguely sensed but never rigorously tested: the faster crickets chirp, the warmer it is. He sat outside on summer evenings, counted chirps, checked his thermometer, and eventually derived a simple linear equation relating the two. The result was elegant enough to feel like a magic trick.

His formula applies specifically to the snowy tree cricket (Oecanthus fultoni), a pale, almost translucent species common across North America. Unlike the louder field crickets most people imagine, snowy tree crickets produce a soft, rhythmic pulse — and because they are cold-blooded, their chirp rate is almost entirely governed by ambient temperature. The warmer it is, the faster their muscles contract, and the faster they stridulate (rub their wings together to produce sound). Dolbear found that counting chirps over 15 seconds and adding 40 gives a remarkably accurate Fahrenheit reading — accurate to within 1–2°F under good conditions.

The law sat quietly in naturalist literature for decades before finding broader fame. It was popularized in the 20th century as a curiosity — a piece of folk science that actually works. The underlying biology is real: enzymatic reaction rates in insects scale with temperature, making crickets living thermometers. The formula breaks down below about 55°F (crickets go silent) and above about 95°F (heat stress disrupts the linearity), but within that window, it is one of the more poetic physical laws you're likely to encounter.
